## Swedish model in dental care

What is the globally most widespread non-communicable disease? Cancer? - close. Cardiovascular diseases? Pretty close, just look a bit higher up. Try to spot stains.
Yes, there it is - dental caries!
<!--more-->
According to the World Health Organisation, there are 2 billion people with permanent teeth, and 510 million children with deciduous (first) teeth are suffering from dental caries or tooth decay. Caries affects people’s confidence, school and work attendance and emotional and mental well-being. Considering these aspects, the WHO suggested a [global oral health policy and action plan](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240090538) for dental caries.
As an Azerbaijani dentist in Sweden, during my adaptation to the Swedish dental care system, I encountered such information: Sweden had only 3% of the total toothless among people older than 50 years. In addition, Sweden also fulfills the aim of the WHO, as people who are older than 80 years have [20 remaining teeth](https://www.internetodontologi.se/protetik/tandloshet/). Numbers were fantastic, so I started to investigate how the dental care system works and which oral health policies are implemented in Sweden.

## Sugar Taxation

Although caries is a multifactorial disease, sugar intake plays a big role. Do Swedes consume less sugar? In fact, Swedes are among [the world’s largest consumers of candy](https://www.business-sweden.com/try-swedish/news-and-insights/sweet-tooth-for-swedish-organic-candy-and-snacks/#:~:text=Swedes%20like%20candy!,service%20pick%20and%20mix%20candy), eating approximately 15 kilograms per person each year. Besides, it is less unlikely to expect from a nation that has a “fika”(“can take place at any time of the day and typically includes freshly brewed coffee served with a sweet pastry, most commonly cinnamon buns”) culture. As the WHO has stated that [taxation of sugar-sweetened beverages](https://iris.who.int/server/api/core/bitstreams/67422ae3-43b3-490d-8d46-03b07932401e/content) should be included in a comprehensive set of measures aimed at addressing dental caries, it is still rejected by the Swedish Tax Committee (Skatteverket). The legitimacy of a sugar tax depends on proving that sugar itself is harmful. Similar to alcohol taxation, public acceptance would likely increase if sugar’s health risks were clearly demonstrated, though this is difficult since health effects often result from multiple factors. Additionaly, [there is another question](https://lup.lub.lu.se/luur/download?func=downloadFile&recordOId=8875123&fileOId=8881931) that Sweden considers: whether it is the state’s role to influence people’s consumption?

## Community Water Fluoridation

When it comes to dental caries, another most considerable field is fluoride intake. Water fluoridation as a key strategy against caries that has been implemented since 1940. Today, Community Water Fluoridation (optimal concentration-1mg/l) is currently practised in about 25 countries. However, since 1971, when the Swedish parliament, by 137 votes to 126, repealed the law permitting the addition of fluoride to drinking water, water fluoridation has no longer been allowed in Sweden. The main reason for not proposing fluoridation of drinking water was that it was seen as an infringement on individual freedom of choice.

## Dental Care in Sweden

As a World Dental Federation member country, Sweden’s oral health policy focuses on early prevention of caries. Sweden has one of the most well-developed dental care systems in the world, particularly in paediatric dentistry. Dental care is provided [free of charge](https://www.1177.se/Skane/sa-fungerar-varden/kostnader-och-ersattningar/kostnader-och-ersattningar-inom-tandvard/tandvard-nar-du-ar-over-19-ar---statligt-tandvardsstod/#section-18055) for all individuals until the year they turn 19, ensuring early access to preventive and restorative services. During this period, children receive regular dental check-ups, preventive care, fillings, and other basic treatments free of charge. This policy helps promote prevention and encourages the development of good oral hygiene habits from an early age. From the age of 20, individuals begin to pay for dental care but receive financial support through the national dental care subsidy system. Financial support for dental care consists of 3 parts:

- General Dental Care Allowance (Allmant tandvardsbidrag): This is an annual state subsidy for dental care, the amount depends on age: 600 SEK for individuals aged 20–23 and 67+, and 300 SEK for those aged 24–65. The allowance is renewed every year on July 1, can be saved for up to two years, and is deducted directly from the cost of dental visits or treatments.
- Special dental care allowance (Sarskild tandvardsbidrag): This support is provided to individuals with specific diseases or functional impairments that affect oral health. It amounts to 1,000 SEK every six months and is intended mainly for people with chronic conditions or medical issues impacting dental health.
- High-Cost Protection Scheme (Högkostnadsskydd)
To reduce the financial burden of dental care, the state covers part of the
treatment costs. Patients pay 100% up to 3,000 SEK, 50% between 3,001 and 15,000 SEK, and 15% for costs above 15,000 SEK, while the state covers the remainder. This scheme applies within 12 months.
There is also Additional Support for Older Adults: Individuals aged 67 and over are eligible for additional high-cost protection. Under this scheme, patients pay only
 about 10% of the cost for many treatments, while the remaining amount is covered by the state.
This example of Swedish oral health policy shows that, as a macro-level factor that influences oral health, national oral health policies play a crucial role by guiding decisions aimed at improving population health. Comprehensive policies enhance access to oral health services and resources while also supporting the development of guidelines that promote high-quality and accessible healthcare systems.
