## Background

What is going to happen when a dentist puts 3 people in a WhatsApp group and promotes to them to use dental floss daily for 10 days?- This was my course assignment for the Health Promotion course. Before starting, let's first understand what health promotion is.
<!--more-->
"[Health promotion is the process of enabling people to increase control over, and to improve their health](https://www.who.int/health-topics/health-promotion#tab=tab_1)." According to the World Health Organization, health is not merely the absence of disease but a resource that enables individuals to achieve their goals, meet their needs, and adapt to their environment. Therefore, health promotion is not limited to healthcare services but encompasses a broader approach that supports overall well-being.

### The Ottawa Charter

On November 21st, 1986, the First International Conference on *Health Promotion*, held in Ottawa, Canada, presented the Ottawa Charter, a framework for action aimed at achieving the goal of “Health for All” by the year 2000 and beyond.It emphasizes that health is not solely the responsibility of the healthcare system but a shared responsibility of society as a whole and various sectors, thereby laying the foundation for modern health promotion approaches.

### The Uppstream Apparoach

The upstream approach focuses on prevent people to become ill instead of treatment of people after they got disease. Although it is important to treat the diseases, the more efficient approach is to eliminate the factors that make people ill.

## The Project

As a dentist I took this approach as a start point for my project and applied it to the usage of dental floss since it is the main part of preventive stomatology. I have observed in my own professional practice that my patients lack information about dental flossing, how it is used, and its health effects. While dental care and oral health affect the body’s overall health status, especially considering cardiovascular diseases, using dental floss daily is an important health promotion intervention.

The ADA and the American Academy of Periodontology continue to recommend daily flossing as a vital step in preventing gingivitis and maintaining long-term oral health. However, according to The Delta Dental Oral Health and Well-Being Survey, 19% of adults reported that they have never used dental floss.

Thus, this project aimed to encourage people to use dental floss daily in their hygiene routine.

### Choice of Theories

At first I made a questionnaire  in order to choose the theoretical basis for my project and according the answers I chose two behavioural theories:

*The Health Belief Model* is used as a promotion theory to create the perceived susceptibility and perceived severity by sharing the academic article about flossing in a WhatsApp group in order to increase awareness of the risks associated with poor oral hygiene, such as gum disease and plaque accumulation. Although the Health Belief Model was useful for increasing awareness of oral health risks and motivating preventive behaviour, it mainly focused on disease prevention and individual risk perception. This partly conflicted with salutogenic health promotion principles, which emphasize strengths, empowerment, and the promotion of positive wellbeing rather than fear of disease. Thus, the intervention balanced both approaches by not only underlining the risks of incomplete oral hygiene routines but also encouraging participants to develop healthy daily routines and self-management skills.

*The Theory of Planned Behaviour* guided the social and behavioural aspects of the intervention. The WhatsApp group with three participants was designed to create social support and accountability, which relates to the TPB concept of subjective norms. Seeing others participate in the ten-day flossing challenge could encourage participants to continue the behaviour themselves. Daily interaction and reminders also supported perceived behavioural control by helping participants feel more capable of maintaining the behaviour consistently. The intervention, therefore, attempted to strengthen behavioural intention through both information and social influence.

[The intervention also adopted the principles of the WHO](https://www.who.int/news/item/26-05-2024-who-releases-global-strategy-and-action-plan-on-oral-health) oral health policy, which focused on early disease prevention, promotion of oral health, education, and self-care. Encouraging flossing aimed to support preventive oral health practices rather than treatment after disease develops.

### Program Description

[The Theory of Change framework](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24996765/) guided implementation by clarifying how the activities were expected to lead to behaviour change. The intervention assumed that providing evidence-based information, reminders, and social encouragement through WhatsApp would increase flossing behaviour during the 10 days. This short-term behaviour change was expected to contribute to improved oral hygiene awareness and potentially support healthier long-term habits. The use of WhatsApp also reflected implementation considerations because it was a low-cost, accessible, and familiar communication platform that allowed easy daily interaction.

![Program Description](/img/posts/when-dentist-trades-drill-for-behavioral-theories/program-description.jpeg)

Sending academic article about the importance of flossing is useless if participants do not know how to floss properly without damaging their gums. It leads to the ***fidelity challenge***. To address this skill gap, firstly, I sent them a little questionnaire, and according to their answers- none of them knew the right flossing technique- they were provided with step-by-step instructional YouTube videos.

### Limitations

The theories mainly focused on the long-term process in behavioural changes, whereas the intervention duration was limited to measuring habit formation or sustained behavioural outcomes.

Another limitation is that theories are not sufficient to determine all the factors that influence the behaviour, such as access to dental care, socioeconomic differences, or cultural attitudes toward oral hygiene.

In addition, whole intervention-implementation process relied mainly on information sharing and self-reporting. Knowledge alone does not always produce lasting behaviour change. Alternative approaches could have included practical demonstrations or habit-tracking tools to strengthen engagement and self-efficacy. In addition, involving a larger and more diverse group of participants would have improved the feasibility and generalizability of the intervention.
